Section 4.2: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Abstract: This section explores the symbiotic relationship between differential and integral calculus. We shall demonstrate that differentiation and integration are inverse operations, a discovery that revolutionized the field of mathematical analysis in the 17th century.

1. Introduction to Derivatives

The derivative of a function $f$ at a point $x$ is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of the function at that point. Formally, it is defined as the limit of the difference quotient as the increment approaches zero:

f'(x) = lim_{Δx → 0} [ f(x + Δx) - f(x) ] / Δx

According to the Mean Value Theorem, for any function that is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, there exists at least one $c$ in the interval such that the instantaneous rate of change equals the average rate of change over the interval.

2. Linear Approximation

In applied physics, linear approximations are utilized to simplify complex transcendental functions. For a function $f(x)$ near a point $a$, the linearization is given by:

L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)

This method provides a high degree of accuracy for values in the immediate vicinity of the anchor point $a$, provided that the second derivative $f''(a)$ is not excessively large [Leibniz et al, 1684].
